# The Rare-Earth Squeeze: How China's Export Halt to Japan Is Repricing Europe's EV and Defense Supply Chains

- China's de facto export ban on **dysprosium, terbium, yttrium and gallium to Japan** — zero shipments since December 2025 according to Japan Times and Discovery Alert — has now widened into the most consequential industrial choke-point of the 2025-2026 cycle, and Europe is downstream of the same chain. EU Parliament analysis puts **roughly 80% of European industrial firms within three supply-chain hops** of Chinese rare-earth inputs.
- The price asymmetry is no longer about cost — it is about **availability**. Refined heavy rare earths now trade at a **6× premium outside China** (EU Parliament think-tank, November 2025), and **fewer than 25% of pending Chinese export licenses are being approved** for non-Chinese end-users. Mitsubishi Heavy has been **named explicitly in dual-use end-use reviews** (CSIS), making clear that the gating is not commercial — it is geopolitical.
- For Central Europe, the exposure is concentrated and growing. **CATL Debrecen** is targeting 40 GWh of battery output and a Responsible Sourcing of Critical Inputs (RSCI) framework rollout in May 2026 (Budapest Business Journal; Electrification Solutions). **BYD Szeged** is building toward a 200,000-vehicle annual run rate in Q2 2026 (IAA Mobility; Carnewschina). **Evoring** is hiring 450 in a magnet-adjacent role (About Hungary, Szijjártó speech February 2026). **Suzuki Esztergom** has already experienced rare-earth-linked production stops (MarketScreener). The Hungarian-Slovak automotive corridor is now sitting directly on the failure mode.

The Japanese rare-earth halt is the most concrete signal in eighteen months that the export-control toolkit Beijing built in 2023-2024 is operationally ready and politically deployable. The May 2026 visit by Japanese trade chief **Akazawa to Beijing** (Discovery Alert) is the diplomatic top-line; the real story is what happens to European industrial planning when the same toolkit faces west.

The conversation below is a composite synthesis of off-record briefings and on-record interviews with three figures across the European industrial-policy ecosystem — edited for clarity, structured the way decision-makers actually triage a supply-chain disruption when the disruption is geopolitical rather than commercial.

The voices:

- **Anneliese K.**, senior fellow at a Brussels-based industrial-policy think tank focused on critical-raw-materials regulation.
- **Hiroshi T.**, a former Japanese trade negotiator with portfolios spanning rare-earth supply security and dual-use export controls.
- **Márton B.**, head of supply-chain operations at a tier-1 automotive contract manufacturer with facilities in Hungary and Slovakia.

---

## What Actually Changed in December 2025

**Q: For readers who only see this story in headlines — what is the substantive change?**

**Hiroshi T.:** What changed is the operational tempo. Until late 2024, Chinese rare-earth export controls were a regulatory mechanism that was applied selectively and slowly. Between December 2025 and now, **shipments of dysprosium, terbium, yttrium, and gallium to Japan have been zero**. That is not a slowdown — that is a halt. Discovery Alert tracked the line items; Japan Times confirmed the operational reality on the ground.

**Anneliese K.:** And the licensing data tells you it is policy, not paperwork. EU Parliament analysis from the November think-tank cycle shows **fewer than 25% of pending export licenses are being approved**. That is a deliberate choke, not a processing delay.

**Márton B.:** From our side of the chain, we found out the same way we always do — through allocation cuts from our magnet suppliers in Q1 2026. Nobody calls you. You just receive 40% of what you ordered, and the rest is in a queue with no resolution date.

---

## Why Mitsubishi Heavy Matters

**Q: CSIS named Mitsubishi Heavy specifically. Why is that the data point that matters?**

**Hiroshi T.:** Because Mitsubishi Heavy is a dual-use entity in everything but legal classification. Their portfolio touches naval propulsion, aerospace components, energy infrastructure, and defense subcontracting. When the Chinese end-use review apparatus names Mitsubishi Heavy explicitly, they are signaling that the gating logic is national-security, not commercial. That changes what every other Japanese industrial buyer has to assume about their own end-use disclosures going forward.

**Anneliese K.:** And it changes what *European* buyers have to assume. If Mitsubishi Heavy is being denied because of dual-use exposure, then every European prime that does any defense subcontracting is now in the same risk category. The list of European industrial firms that have zero defense-adjacent exposure is very short.

---

## The 6× Premium and What It Hides

**Q: A 6× premium outside China sounds like the kind of price signal that should attract supply. Why isn't it?**

**Anneliese K.:** Because the premium is not about price discovery — it is about scarcity in non-Chinese refining capacity. **EU Parliament's November think-tank report** put the premium at roughly 6× for heavy rare-earth refined products. But the bottleneck is not the mine. It is the separation and refining stage, which China has spent twenty years building monopoly capacity in. A 6× premium does not buy you a new refinery — it buys you a license to wait three to five years for one to come online.

**Hiroshi T.:** Japan understood this earlier than the EU. After the 2010 episode, Japan funded Lynas through its trading houses precisely to avoid this. But that did not solve the heavy rare-earth problem — only the light. Dysprosium and terbium are the choke points now, and Lynas's heavy-rare-earth capacity is still ramping.

**Márton B.:** For a buyer like us, the 6× premium also assumes the material is available *at any price*. For some heavy rare-earth grades right now, it is not.

---

## The 80% Number — Why Europe Is Downstream

**Q: EU Parliament said 80% of European industrial firms sit within three supply-chain hops of Chinese rare-earth inputs. What does that actually mean operationally?**

**Anneliese K.:** It means that even firms that have done extensive supply-chain diversification work on their **direct** suppliers are still exposed through their **suppliers' suppliers**. The magnet inside a brushless motor inside a HVAC system inside a building delivered to a German construction firm is three hops. The catalytic converter inside an exhaust system inside a vehicle is the same. The 80% figure is what diligence looks like when you actually trace down to the input material, not just the tier-1 component.

**Márton B.:** And the disclosure does not exist at the sub-tier level for most contracts. When we ask a magnet supplier where their dysprosium came from, the honest answer is often "we do not know — our supplier does not tell us." That is the data gap that becomes a strategic problem when the geopolitics changes.

## What Akazawa's Beijing Visit Tells Us

**Q: Akazawa went to Beijing in May 2026. Is that a negotiation, or political theater?**

**Hiroshi T.:** It is a negotiation that both sides expect will fail, conducted publicly so that both sides can demonstrate to their domestic audiences that they tried. Akazawa is professional, the meetings will be substantive, but Beijing's leverage is structural. They do not need a deal — they have already proven the export-control toolkit works. Japan needs the deal more than China does, which is exactly why China can extract terms or simply let the talks run out the clock.

**Anneliese K.:** And the EU is watching this carefully because the same dynamic will apply when it is our turn. Brussels has been preparing the Critical Raw Materials Act, the new minerals partnerships in Australia, Canada, Vietnam. But all of that is multi-year buildout. The Japanese precedent shows that China can apply pressure on a quarter-by-quarter basis. The mismatch in timelines is the entire problem.

## Central Europe Specifically — The Hungarian Exposure

**Q: Hungary's automotive build-out is the most exposed Central European position. Walk me through it.**

**Márton B.:** Hungary has positioned itself as the EV-supplychain bridge between Chinese capital and European demand. **CATL is building toward 40 GWh of battery capacity in Debrecen, with their RSCI — Responsible Sourcing of Critical Inputs — framework targeted for May 2026 according to BBJ and Electrification Solutions reporting.** That framework matters because it is the formal mechanism through which CATL Debrecen would be expected to source rare-earth-bearing inputs in a compliant way under EU rules. The framework launching this month is timed exactly when the Japanese signal is hottest.

**Anneliese K.:** And the framework only works if the inputs are actually available. RSCI compliance can certify what is sourced; it cannot conjure dysprosium that is not being shipped.

**Márton B.:** **BYD Szeged** is building toward a **200,000-vehicle annual run rate in Q2 2026** — IAA Mobility and Carnewschina coverage confirms the timeline. **Evoring announced 450 jobs in a magnet-adjacent role** in February 2026, according to About Hungary's coverage of Szijjártó's announcement. And **Suzuki Esztergom has already taken production stops linked to rare-earth supply** per MarketScreener. So the Hungarian-Slovak automotive corridor is now sitting on a stack of new EV capacity ramping into a heavy-rare-earth choke. That is the actual exposure picture.

---

## What Does a Realistic Mitigation Look Like

**Q: For a European automotive prime today — what actually mitigates this in the next 12 months?**

**Márton B.:** In twelve months, very little structurally. You can do three things: stock buffer, alternate motor designs that reduce or eliminate heavy-rare-earth content, and supplier diversification toward non-Chinese magnet producers. Stock buffer is the only one that helps inside twelve months. The other two are 24-to-48-month projects.

**Anneliese K.:** And stock buffer is expensive. Capital sits in warehouse inventory rather than working in the business. For a Hungarian OEM running on contract-manufacturing margins, the cost is meaningful.

**Hiroshi T.:** Japan's twelve-month answer was the same — buffer, then bridge. The bridge is what we are still building fifteen years later.

---

## Where the Defense Angle Goes

**Q: The Mitsubishi Heavy disclosure pulled the defense industrial base into the rare-earth conversation. How does this reshape European defense procurement?**

**Hiroshi T.:** Quickly. If a European prime wins a tender for radar, fighter avionics, naval propulsion, missile guidance — any platform with permanent-magnet content — the question of *where the dysprosium came from* now sits on the contract sheet. Government buyers are starting to require sourcing disclosures. That changes which suppliers can credibly bid.

**Anneliese K.:** And the European Defence Fund is starting to write critical-minerals language into the eligibility criteria. The Critical Raw Materials Act framework is becoming load-bearing for defense procurement, not just industrial policy.

---

## What This Means for Capital Allocation

**Q: For an investor watching this story — what is the realistic position?**

**Anneliese K.:** The investable thesis is non-Chinese refining capacity. Mines are not the bottleneck; refining is. Lynas in Australia, MP Materials in the US, the various Canadian projects under development. Selectively, the Vietnamese and African refining build-outs. These are not all near-term equity stories — many are five-year project finance plays. But the structural demand is locked in.

**Hiroshi T.:** And the demand side — the auto OEMs that have diversified out of heavy-rare-earth motor designs, the defense primes with credible sourcing disclosure. Those are the names that survive the squeeze.

**Márton B.:** From the operator side, the question is who has buffer and who does not. The OEMs that built strategic inventory in 2024-2025 are surviving this round. The ones that did not are doing emergency procurement at the 6× price — when they can find supply at all.

---

## The Hardest Question

**Q: Is there a credible scenario where this de-escalates inside 2026?**

**Hiroshi T.:** No. The toolkit is built, it has been demonstrated, and there is no political incentive for Beijing to put it down. Even if the Japan-specific halt loosens, the broader export-control regime is now permanent infrastructure. The question is which other end-users get caught in it next.

**Anneliese K.:** The most likely de-escalation is partial — specific licenses for specific civilian end-uses granted in return for specific concessions. That is what the Akazawa visit is for. But the structural picture does not change.

**Márton B.:** From the factory floor, we are planning for the structural picture. The optimism case is that we get an additional three months of supply at some grades. The realism case is that we engineer the dysprosium out of the next platform.
