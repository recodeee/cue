# What Serious Capital Wants in Crypto in 2026

- Serious investors are skipping narratives and prioritizing tokenized real-world assets, regulated stablecoins, institutional execution venues, and the AI-compute and energy stack that now sits behind every credible crypto thesis.
- Allocators still face the same operational friction they did in 2023 — custody, banking access, mandate gaps, legal structure, due diligence depth, and reputation risk inside their own investment committees.
- Platforms that move capital in 2026 win by keeping investors in control of the funds, surfacing real-time strategy and risk data, documenting trades in a form a compliance officer can sign off on, and routing flow through licensed exchanges rather than counterparty wallets.

The shape of crypto allocation has changed twice in eighteen months. First when DeepSeek and the Chinese model labs forced the entire AI-compute curve into a permanent price war — most recently this morning, when DeepSeek announced another round of permanent price cuts to its inference API, dragging the cost-to-serve narrative under every Nvidia bull's seat. And second when Bank of America's Michael Hartnett told clients this was "the biggest bubble since the railway bubble," advising patience over panic but flagging two specific signals he wants to see before the sell-off.

The conversation below is a composite synthesis of dialogues across Hong Kong, Singapore, Zurich and New York over the last six weeks — three voices, edited for clarity, structured the way allocators actually think when capital is on the table.

The voices:

- **Mira T.**, principal at an Asia-based multi-family office (HKD-denominated book, ~USD 2.4B AUM, less than 5% in digital assets).
- **Daniel R.**, partner at a US generalist venture firm now running a dedicated crypto and AI-infrastructure sleeve.
- **Sofia L.**, head of digital-asset prime services at a European bank-licensed broker.

## What Serious Investors Want in 2026

**Q: What does the capital journey into crypto look like from your side of the desk?**

**Mira T.:** It is still a thorny road, but the rocks have changed shape. In 2023 the question was "can we even custody this." In 2026 the question is "can we hold this through a drawdown without a board meeting." Family offices here are conservative — most of our clients sit under 5% digital assets, and inside that envelope, 70% is in BTC, ETH, and ETFs. The interesting capital is the 30% that wants exposure to something that compounds.

**Daniel R.:** The capital journey now looks much more like a real investment process. Investors ask for cash flows, treasury policy, jurisdiction of issuance, the legal wrapper, who the auditor is. A few years ago many investors entered crypto for exposure and upside. Today they are looking for real demand and a better understanding of how liquidity actually works inside a position.

**Sofia L.:** From the prime side, what changed is that allocators now arrive with a thesis instead of a budget. They tell us "we want yield on stablecoins, basis trade, perpetuals funding, with monthly reporting and no rehypothecation." That is a level of specificity we did not see two years ago.

**Q: What kind of crypto opportunities are serious investors looking for in 2026?**

**Daniel R.:** The strongest interest is in sectors where crypto solves a problem the traditional system cannot — tokenization of private credit, regulated stablecoins as a settlement layer, institutional execution venues, and the intersection of AI compute, power, and on-chain settlement. The DeepSeek price-cut cycle is forcing everyone to re-underwrite the compute layer. Capital that used to chase L1 narratives is now sitting on data-center and energy theses with a crypto settlement tail.

**Mira T.:** It also depends on the region. In Asia, tokenized RWAs are the dominant request. Family offices like that they are regulated, that they have substance underneath, and that the legal protections look like things they already understand. Hedge funds focused on quant crypto strategies are the next layer — but only the licensed ones.

**Sofia L.:** And on the operational side, demand is for the things that look like prime brokerage. Cross-margining, off-exchange settlement, segregated custody — the boring parts. The exotic parts are not what is closing tickets right now.

## What Happens When Capital Enters a Platform

**Q: When a family office allocates into an institutional crypto platform in 2026, what actually happens operationally?**

**Sofia L.:** In our case, the client opens an account in their own name on a regulated exchange or with our licensed entity. They retain title to the assets. We provide the strategy layer, the risk model, and the reporting. They keep custody. That separation is non-negotiable for any allocator we work with — capital does not leave the client's name.

**Daniel R.:** On the venture side it is different — capital does leave, but into a regulated fund vehicle with a third-party administrator, an auditor, and a custodian we have already diligenced. The vehicle does the trading; the LP gets a quarterly report. That is the structure 90% of new institutional money is using.

**Mira T.:** For our clients the choice is between segregated subaccounts on a licensed exchange — where we control keys via API permissions — and a fully delegated mandate inside a fund vehicle. The first is more popular because it is auditable in real time. We can see every position, every fill, every transfer. There is no monthly waiting.

## What Gives Investors Confidence

**Q: What gives investors enough confidence to deploy capital into crypto today?**

**Daniel R.:** Confidence comes from track record under stress. Anyone can show a great Q1 in a bull market. The investable teams are the ones that survived 2022, 2023, the regulator turn in 2024, and the AI-compute repricing in 2025. We look for clean cap tables, conservative treasury policy, and a clear path to revenue that does not depend on token emissions.

**Mira T.:** For us it is documentation. A strategy we can read. A risk model we can plug numbers into. Independent verification of trades. The team's regulatory standing in our jurisdiction. None of that is glamorous, but it is what gets through our investment committee.

**Sofia L.:** And the right question is not "can this team make money in a good market." The right question is "can this team survive the market they did not expect." We have seen too many platforms that were perfect in calm conditions and then disappeared the week conditions changed.

## How Platforms Help Allocators Enter Crypto

**Q: How do modern platforms compress the operational friction for allocators?**

**Sofia L.:** By owning the parts allocators do not want to own. We carry the regulatory licenses, the banking relationships, the custody integrations, and the reporting infrastructure. The client gets API access into a system that already speaks the language their compliance team speaks. That is the entire value proposition.

**Daniel R.:** And on the strategy side, the platforms that win are documenting their methodology — drawdown windows, position-sizing logic, leverage caps — in a way that an outside reviewer can audit. Pulse-style allocator platforms running on exchange subaccounts are doing this well: the client sees historical data, real-time positions, and an automated execution layer, and the funds never leave their name.

**Mira T.:** The platforms also need to think about the journey after the first allocation. A first ticket is a test. The platform needs lower friction for the second ticket, ecosystem access, lower commissions, early product access. Otherwise the client moves capital elsewhere the moment they have proof the strategy works.

## Where Capital Gets Stuck

**Q: Where does capital get stuck between investor interest and actual deployment?**

**Daniel R.:** Four places. Custody — institutions will not hand assets to a counterparty they cannot audit. Mandate — most traditional funds simply do not have a crypto allocation bucket, and creating one takes board approval and months of legal. Due diligence — most crypto products fail institutional review because the strategy is poorly documented or has not been independently validated. Sizing — without a real risk model, allocators cannot decide how much capital to deploy, so they default to zero or to a token position.

**Sofia L.:** Banking is the underrated one. Try opening a new corporate account in Hong Kong, Frankfurt, or Singapore and telling the bank you intend to wire significant capital to a digital-asset venue. You will be rejected even if every counterparty in the chain is licensed. Stablecoin rails — regulated, audited, with proper attestation — are quietly solving more of this than people realize.

**Mira T.:** And inside the institution itself, the most expensive blocker is reputational. Every allocation has to go through committees. There is still limited trust in information that comes from crypto-native sources, especially for new platforms. Even when interest is real, no one wants to be the first person on the committee to put their name on the deal. The cost shows up as allocations that keep getting pushed to the next quarter.

## Why Allocators Are Watching the AI-Compute Stack

**Q: How does the DeepSeek price cycle and the AI-compute repricing change the crypto allocation conversation?**

**Daniel R.:** It changes the whole frame. When DeepSeek can serve inference at the prices they announced this morning, the unit economics of every AI-compute thesis get repriced. That hits Nvidia, it hits hyperscalers, it hits the financing structures underneath data-center buildouts. And it pulls capital toward the next layer — distributed compute markets, energy-backed settlement, on-chain compute marketplaces. That is the crypto sleeve of the AI thesis.

**Mira T.:** Asian allocators are particularly active here because they sit close to both the AI labs and the energy supply chains. We are seeing more crossover questions — "if we are taking compute exposure, can we settle it on-chain, can we collateralize the contract, can we get the cash flow inside a tokenized vehicle." Those questions did not exist a year ago.

**Sofia L.:** And on the markets side, Hartnett's "biggest bubble since the railway bubble" framing matters more than people think. He is not saying sell — he is saying wait for two signals, and until then this rally keeps running. That posture is what allocators are pricing in. They want exposure but with downside structure. That favors tokenized-credit and yield-bearing stablecoin products over directional spot positions.

## Control, Reporting, and Transparency

**Q: How important is operational transparency when investors assess crypto opportunities?**

**Sofia L.:** It is the entire game. In crypto, investors can accept risk. They cannot accept a black box. We provide a full audit log of every trade, every transfer, every signature. The numbers in the monthly report match the on-chain history, line for line. If they do not, the platform does not get a second allocation.

**Daniel R.:** Transparency also changes the negotiation. When the strategy is fully documented and on-chain, the conversation moves from "trust us" to "here is the data, audit it yourself." That collapses the diligence timeline from months to weeks.

**Mira T.:** And it changes who can sign off. A clear, documented strategy can get through a family-office IC meeting in one session. An opaque one needs three meetings and an outside consultant. The cost difference is real.

## Where Demand Goes Next

**Q: Which crypto sectors are most likely to attract serious capital in 2026?**

**Daniel R.:** Tokenization of private credit and trade finance. Regulated stablecoins as a payments rail, especially across the US, EU, Hong Kong, and UAE frameworks now in place. Institutional execution venues with proper prime brokerage. And the AI-compute and energy crossover — that is where the new theses are being written.

**Mira T.:** Tokenized RWAs first, then yield-bearing stablecoin products, then quant trading strategies on licensed venues. In that order. The retail-style narratives are gone for serious capital.

**Sofia L.:** From our flow, the strongest growth is in basis trade, options, and structured products on stablecoin collateral. Boring instruments doing high volume. That is what mature capital looks like.

**Q: Where do you expect the strongest allocator demand over the next year?**

**Mira T.:** Asia. Family offices and funds of funds that already understand quant-allocation workflows. The US is moving but the regulatory picture is still uneven enough that committee approvals lag.

**Daniel R.:** US, but specifically the endowments and pensions that have been waiting on regulatory clarity. Once the first one allocates publicly, the second follows in 60 days. That cascade is queued up.

**Sofia L.:** Europe is catching up faster than most people realize. MiCA and the bank-licensed digital-asset infrastructure means German and Swiss allocators are now doing things they could not do twelve months ago.

## The Most Expensive Blocker

**Q: What is the most expensive blocker for institutional capital entering crypto today?**

**Mira T.:** Reputational risk inside the institution. Not the asset, not the regulator — the room. Every allocation passes through committees where someone has to stake their name on it. Until that someone is comfortable being the first, the capital stays in cash.

**Daniel R.:** The biggest hidden cost is the operational burden behind every decision. Serious investors need custody, compliance, reporting, risk control, liquidity planning, token-unlock awareness. Most platforms underestimate how much of the value they provide is the back office, not the strategy.

**Sofia L.:** Banking infrastructure. The rails are still the rate-limiting step for almost every institutional flow. Compliant stablecoin systems are solving this faster than anyone expected, and that will be the unlock for the next wave.

---

## Trust Comes First

**Q: What needs to improve first for more capital to enter crypto?**

**Sofia L.:** Trust. Cleaner risk management, more reliable systems around custody and compliance, and reporting that lets capital enter, operate, and exit safely. Trust is the new gold.

**Mira T.:** Substance. The era of pitching narratives is over. Allocators want substance — a regulated vehicle, a real cash flow, a legal structure that protects them. Whatever does not have substance underneath will not get a ticket from us.

**Daniel R.:** Track record under stress. The platforms and protocols that survive the next drawdown will define the next decade of institutional crypto. The ones that do not will be remembered as the railway companies that did not make it past 1847. Hartnett's framing was harsher than the market wanted to hear — but it is the right one to underwrite to.
